# Niche guide

Picking the right "source channel" is 70% of whether your stitched videos land. The hook half decides if someone keeps watching long enough to see your end-clip. Your CTA can be perfect, if the hook doesn't grab them in the first 0.8 seconds, they're gone.

## What makes a good source channel

1. **Visual immediacy**, you understand what's happening within one frame. No setup, no slow build.
2. **Compulsion to keep watching**, the brain wants to see how it resolves. Wood being planed, slime being squished, a domino chain falling.
3. **Loops well**, short enough that ~5 seconds feels like the natural length, not a chunk of something longer.
4. **No talking-head intro**, voice-over kills retention on a stitched edit. Pure visual is better.
5. **High volume**, you want a channel with hundreds of Shorts so you have variety.

## Niches that work well

### 🧪 3D explainer animations

Physics demos, mechanism breakdowns, "how stuff actually works" explainers with cinematic 3D.

Strong because curiosity is one of the strongest hooks on YouTube. People literally cannot scroll past a "how does this work" question.

### ✨ Satisfying videos

Cleaning, slime, pressure-washing, marble runs, kinetic sand, food assembly, factory production lines.

Strong because the brain releases dopamine on a 1-2 second loop. Built-in retention even if your CTA is bad.

### 💃 Female dancing

High-energy compilation channels with sharp choreography or trend-based dance edits.

Strong because attention is automatic. Caveat: more competitive niche, more re-uploaded content, more copyright risk. Pick channels that license or own the dance footage.

### 🪚 Skill compilations

Woodworking, knife restoration, lathe work, sushi rolling, plating, tempura, sports trick shots.

Strong because "ooh, I want to be able to do that" is a universal feeling.

### 🎬 Movie / TV clips

⚠ Skip. High copyright risk. Getting your channel struck is borderline guaranteed at scale.

### 🐈 Animals

Works but saturated. Differentiate with something unusual (exotic pets, training shorts, rescue stories).

## Niche × CTA matching

The CTA in your end-clip should feel emotionally connected to the source. If the source is satisfying woodwork and your CTA is "track your peptide intake with bo," there's a tonal mismatch that costs retention.

| Source niche | CTA tone that works |
|---|---|
| 3D explainer | "stop wondering. start doing." → educational app |
| Satisfying videos | "your habits could feel this clean" → habit/productivity app |
| Female dancing | "imagine your body looking like this in 6 months" → fitness/health app |
| Skill compilation | "you could be the person doing this" → learning/skill app |
| Cooking | "your meals could feel this dialed in" → meal-tracking/recipe app |

If you can't match the tone, the issue might be that your app and your chosen niche don't actually go together. Pick a different niche.

## How to find a channel

Search YouTube for "[niche] shorts" or "[niche] compilation". Open the channel's Shorts tab. Scroll for 30 seconds and ask: would I stop scrolling on this? If no, pick a different channel.

You don't need to credit the channel in your stitched video, but you should respect them. If they ask you to stop, switch channels. It costs you nothing and avoids drama.

## Testing different niches

Run two parallel campaigns. Same hooks, different source channels. After 50 uploads each, compare avg views/hour. Whichever wins, double down on that niche.

Don't shuffle channels mid-campaign, the YouTube algorithm seems to penalize abrupt content switches on a channel.
